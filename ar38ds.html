Older does not equal Wiser: The Truth About Decisions

Why age stopped being a valid argument and what actually drives good decision-making.

Personal Insight
March 2026
5 min read
Thought of the day

Since when did growing older become proof of thinking better?

We have been told this forever. Older means wiser. Experience means authority. Age means right. Today, we are questioning all of it. And honestly, it is long overdue.

Every time, age gets treated as the ultimate measure of maturity in decision-making. And sure, respect to elders, their experience matters. No doubt. But the moment you say that good decisions require experience, a real question hits: how will younger people ever gain that experience if their decisions are constantly controlled or dismissed?

The Breakdown
Point 01

Experience Does Not Equal Good Judgment

From what I have observed, decisions are not proportional to age. People with years of experience are often so conditioned by their past choices that they stop seeing new possibilities entirely. Their mental model gets locked in. Their "wisdom" becomes a filter that blocks fresh thinking.

"It worked before, so it will work now." That one sentence has buried more good ideas than any bad decision ever could.

It might sound harsh, but even adults end up facing lifelong consequences because of their own poor decisions. So clearly, age and experience do not guarantee wisdom. They just guarantee that you have been alive longer.

Point 02

What Decision-Making Actually Depends On

In reality, good decisions depend more on clarity of thought, on awareness, on mindset, and on the personal perspective you carry into a situation. Not on the number of years you have lived.

And honestly, many people will not relate to this. Because if you have never been allowed to make your own decisions, it is easy to believe that someone else should always decide for you. And then you pass that same control down to the next generation. The cycle just continues.

If they are never allowed to choose, never allowed to fail, then when exactly are they supposed to learn?
Point 03

The Sibling Argument That Proves Everything

Let me give you the simplest example. In most homes, during a disagreement between siblings, the elder one often wins. Not because they are right. But because they are older.

"I am older, so you should listen to me." And just like that, logic leaves the room.

This does not stop there. In many families, men dominate decisions simply because they earn or work outside. As if financial contribution automatically means better judgment. But think about it: does earning more really mean thinking better? That idea is not just flawed. It is outdated.

Point 04

Power Is Not the Same as Perspective

And when that control shifts from money to gender, society still justifies it. Not through logic, but through power. The woman's perspective gets dismissed not because it is wrong, but simply because it belongs to her. Her voice gets treated as optional input rather than an actual say in decisions that affect her own life.

But decision-making is not about strength, dominance, or gender. It is a personal instinct, shaped by perspective, not by who holds the most authority in the room. The loudest voice is rarely the most accurate one.

Not everyone starts with perfect judgment. That is exactly why people need the freedom to decide, fail, and learn.
💡

Here Is the Thing Nobody Says Out Loud

If you cannot take responsibility for your own decisions, you have no right to control someone else's life. That is not harsh. That is just logical.

Let experience come naturally, whether it leads to mistakes or growth. Because growth that comes from someone else's choices is not really your growth. It is just borrowed certainty you never actually tested.

It is your life. Your direction. Your call. Value those who genuinely want you to grow, not just everyone who has had influence over you. Because not every contributor deserves to be followed.

So What Is the Final Verdict?

Let us end this belief that decisions come after experience.

Because the truth is, experience comes from decisions. You do not become wise and then decide. You decide, and that is how you become wise.

The next time someone pulls the age card on you, remember: being older just means they have had more time to either learn or ignore the lesson.

Age is a number. Clarity of thought is the actual flex.
